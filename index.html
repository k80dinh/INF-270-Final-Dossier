Systems Work at Cultural Heritage Institutions

INF-270 Specialization Dossier by: Katie Dinh, Isabel Filson, Amorah Schultze, and Nico Townsend

Overview

This dossier is based on an interview with Heather Hart, Vice President for the Digital and Technology department, at The Huntington. According to her profile on the institution's website, Hart "oversees the network of systems and systems architecture that underpin The Huntington's digital infrastructure." In the context of The Huntington, digital systems include library catalogs, digital asset management systems (DAMS), collections management systems (CMS), and financial management systems.

Hart's role falls under the umbrella of information technology and systems design within spaces of cultural stewardship. After the interview and further research, positions within these two specializations are diverse in the types of responsibilities required. In other words, the core duties and type of technological practices these professionals are engaged in are predicated on the goals, size, funding and collection practices of a particular institution. Furthermore, not every GLAM institution will have an official position labeled “IT” or “systems” Rather, these organizations will have roles named with qualifiers like “technology” or “digital” that often require personnel to perform adjacent tasks.

In a library, museum or archive, systems and IT work typically include the development of responsive websites or applications, integrating electronic tools for exhibitions, digital collections management (e.g. assisting with CMS and DAMS), and converting electronic collections into public-facing displays. However, technical knowledge and engagement with systems design is greater for practitioners at lower occupational levels. At the senior level, roles prioritize managerial decision making alongside an extensive understanding of a given organization's digital infrastructure. Regardless, individuals interested in pursuing future careers within this specialization are required to be technologically adept and adaptable. Given the rapidly-evolving nature of digital tools, aspiring professionals should focus on gaining a well-rounded, working-understanding of these utilities as opposed to expertise—whether it be a new hardware, software, or computing language—to creatively apply them to the problems faced at a specific institution.
