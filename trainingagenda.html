Training Agenda

Senior level positions for Digital and Technology Management requires a diverse blend of hard and soft skills that can be utilized across various different softwares and programs utilized in information technology rather than one specific programming language to know. This can make “pathways” into IS less straightforward than a STEM career, which is demonstrated by Heather herself, who was a print journalism and political science major. Rather than targeting a particular degree or set of classes, gaining a broader understanding of the kind of the thought process that goes into fitting different digital systems into a single organizational plan are ideal for this field.

    Organizational knowledge and prioritization over perfection are key to breaking into this field. Managing institutions that may have dozens, if not hundreds of file databases that require preservation plans, ensuring each employee is equipped with specialized, comprehensive knowledge in the various systems and software they use is the goal of the job rather than expecting employees to acquire knowledge in a different area. Preparing employees on structured information systems that can be understood by other teams and advise on digital resources (tables, fields, coding, etc.) that are maximized for usefulness across institutions using categorization, hierarchies, and prioritization.
