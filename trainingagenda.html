Training Agenda

6) Training agenda: In order to enter this profession, what kinds of preparation should someone undertake? Are there particular classes they should take, internships they should pursue, or technical skills they should develop?

Senior-level positions in Digital and Technology Management require a blend of hard and soft skills that can be applied across many different software systems used in information technology rather than expertise in a single programming language. This can make pathways into Information Science less straightforward than traditional STEM careers.

Heather herself, for example, was a print journalism and political science major. Rather than targeting one specific degree path, gaining a broader understanding of how digital systems fit into organizational planning is ideal preparation for this field.

Flexibility in creative problem-solving and collaboration is often more valuable than rigid technical specialization. These abilities are commonly strengthened through project-management training.

Because nearly all aspects of this profession involve coordinating projects across departments, strong organizational management skills are essential.

Institutional Systems & Technologies

Institutions often manage hundreds of databases that require preservation planning and structured organization. The goal is not for every employee to understand every system, but to ensure that information structures are standardized so teams can collaborate effectively.

  • Large institutional databases requiring preservation plans
  • Shared CRM systems for visitor and ticketing management
  • Sierra ILS – library and archival collections
  • ContentDM – digital library repositories
  • AM – circulation system for library cards
  • TMS – museum collections database
  • BGBase – botanical garden database
  • FGIS mapping software
