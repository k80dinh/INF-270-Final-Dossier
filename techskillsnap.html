Technical Skill Snapshot

At the entry level, this specialization in digital and technological work begins with a robust understanding of software, data, and systems necessary to support a library, museum, or archive. We do predict a growing demand for skills in software and programming, however, these may change form and shape as AI and technology continues to advance. Heather advises that software changes quickly, and not to overemphasize these skills. Internet and technology literacy will continue to be important in this specialization, as well as hands-on capabilities when it comes to managing systems. Like Heather shared with us, hands-on technical work in the institutional realm is a regular task for entry-level roles in this specialization. Furthermore, technology skills will remain critical as born-online collections continue to grow and require collection management. Because of the need to digitally preserve cultural heritage materials, as well as make collections available for online access, the need for those with skills in data organization, software, and design may grow. Likewise, cultural competency and communication skills are also critical for cultural preservation work, especially as a leader or manager. These higher-level roles, like Heather’s, exist at the intersection of technical skills, leadership experience, problem-solving capabilities, and experience with cultural heritage institutions, or similar spaces. Hart has identified a few key characteristics for employee training that require flexibility and problem-solving skills over knowledge of specific programs. According to the other identified job applications for IT archival specialty, DAMS programs like Nuxeo QLab, Dante, NDI, Q-SYS, OBS, Blackmagic, Adobe Premier, Mad Mapper, Vectorworks, 22 Miles, discovery platforms like Calisphere, JSTOR, Omeka, and CDNC, and collaboration with existing digital repositories or preservation services like Merritt are all incorporated into system training areas for many archival and museum organizations, depending on their specific institutional needs. Because of the diverse and oftentimes complex software programs that are launched to the public, translational coding skills are important for the longevity of the Huntington’s digital management. This requires a training system that is tractable, specialized, and coordinated, where learning protocols and manuals must be independently developed for each department and centralized by Heather. She references the Huntington One system as a way of streamlining this training agenda, a goal-oriented plan that outlines how the different areas of the Huntington (library, museum, gardens, public services, etc.) creates a shared language for its collections indexing, exhibition planning, and workflow that influences the digital management training disseminated to its employees. It’s a theoretical framework that guides the development of training material for its employees to determine how to classify or compile intellectual content on Huntington’s digital databases that necessitates foresight and guidance from Heather. This aligns with the higher function of her role as an advisor and manager of the OH model, which prioritizes softer skillsets like problem-solving, communication, and the ability to break down material and software for future teams.
