Labor Trends

Entering the Profession & Experience

Heather Hart noted that jobs within her field are rare, and entry-level opportunities are hard to come by. An MLIS or technology-related degree is not necessarily required, and Heather entered the profession in a non-linear path, as someone with a political science background. Experience is more valued, and Hart gained hers through roles in web-design, digital marketing, and collections digitization. With this in mind, aspiring professionals should also look out for internships, volunteer work, and other tech jobs outside of GLAM institutions for experience.

Job Discrimination and Pay Discrepancies

For those who are of marginalized identities, breaking into the technological management side of cultural organizations can prove difficult due to ideas of who should be representing these institutions and barriers of extensive professional experience. From what Hart can offer on the subject, she and others attempt to put forth measures in their hiring process to prevent discrimination and to understand how to recruit candidates from underserved backgrounds. As Hart has mentioned, because of the high desirability and difficulty of obtaining positions serving the technological needs of cultural institutions, the market is competitive. Those who get in tend not to leave their comfortable job. Entry level positions can lead to more reliable and better employment, but tend to result in failed promises of employment mobility. Budget constraints of such institutions are factors to low pay and the high competitiveness of such jobs, since even non- profit organizations need to be able to cover the cost of employees through their organizational earnings. If there are no funds, institutions will not hire. Hart has discussed her frustrations with the current employment market in her field and wishes budget constraints were not so prevalent so people could bargain for better paying jobs.

Market Demands

For IT and systems work in museums generally, Heather has commented upon the increase in layoffs for professionals who work with front-end facing systems, especially after COVID-19 impacted the ability for individuals to visit museums. This includes workers who use technology to improve visitor engagement. Some examples include virtual reality exhibits (VR), digital displays, and interactive kiosks. Although these technologies can improve vistor experiences, integrating them within museums can be logistically challenging. Routine maintenance, purchasing hardware and software, and hiring specialized staff is quite costly (MuseumNext 2023). For institutions impacted by lack of visitors post-pandemic or budget-cuts, front-facing technology specialists are particularly vulnerable to dissmisals.
