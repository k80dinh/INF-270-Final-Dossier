AI Implications

At The Huntington, there are no formal AI guidelines. However, it is Heather’s current task to devise an official document pertaining to the internal use of generative AI. At the moment, she notes that The Huntington is compiling a list of approved tools, exploring privacy implications within a museum setting, and its potential uses. Despite the widespread use of AI over the past few years, the institution has always been slow to adapt to new technology. In the early years of its growing popularity, Heather stated that she would even disable AI features for staff in all technologies purchased from vendors due to concerns for data privacy and an inability for the digital department to monitor all usage. Yet, integrating AI at The Huntington has become unavoidable, especially since there is no longer an option to deactivate these features in purchased systems from external vendors. For example, OrangeLogic is now used as a DAMS, and it has built in AI features.

With this in mind, staff are currently only permitted to utilize functional tools, as opposed to personal tools, and must document why and when AI was employed. Some common uses include transcribing meeting minutes and removing background images for photos of merchandise. These procedures additionally apply to external labor—especially marketers and designers.

She notes that AI has not significantly affected her work. As she occupies a managerial role, human communication, collaboration and negotiation with staff and vendors is a large part of her job responsibilities—tasks she believes that cannot be automated. However, AI can have more impact on technical positions lower in the occupational hierarchy. Especially for metadata specialists, data processors, and in digital asset management, it has been utilized to improve workflow and cut costs—particularly with cataloging, tagging, media scaling, and data cleaning. Although tasks are increasingly automated, human oversight is still considered necessary for quality control (Athey 2024).
